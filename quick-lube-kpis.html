12 quick-lube KPIs
every operator should track.

The oil-change metrics that move a multi-unit business: what each one means and why it matters.

Book a demo Auto-service analytics

Quick-lube is high-volume: a car rolls in, a few decisions get made, it rolls out, hundreds of times a day. Operators who win across locations watch the same numbers the same way at every store. Below are the twelve KPIs multi-unit quick-lube and auto-service operators run on. We skip benchmark numbers, because what matters is your own trend and how each store ranks against the rest of your chain.

myAnalyst reads these straight from your POS and refreshes them every five minutes, so you compare live reality store-to-store instead of rebuilding spreadsheets. New to a term? The glossary defines the language here.

1. Car count

What it is

Vehicles serviced in a period. It is the base volume metric in quick-lube; everything else is a rate applied to it.

Why it matters

Car count tells you whether revenue swings come from traffic or ticket size, and it is the first signal a market has changed. myAnalyst shows it live in LiveStats alongside net sales with year-over-year deltas, so a dip surfaces the same day, not at month-end.

2. Average ticket

What it is

Net sales divided by car count: average dollars per vehicle across everything sold that visit.

Why it matters

This is where service mix, pricing, and upsell discipline land. Two stores with identical car counts can post very different revenue. Track the trend per store and rank stores against each other rather than chasing an outside rule of thumb. myAnalyst surfaces average ticket per location with YoY change built in.

3. ARO (Average Repair Order)

What it is

The average value of a service order. ARO captures how much work beyond the base oil change a store writes per vehicle.

Why it matters

ARO is the clearest measure of upsell and service-mix health. A rising ARO means technicians are inspecting, recommending, and closing the services a customer needs. It is one of the signature Auto Care metrics myAnalyst tracks; see its definition in the glossary.

4. Labor %

What it is

Labor cost as a percentage of net sales: what you spend on people relative to what you bring in.

Why it matters

Labor is the largest controllable cost in most quick-lube shops, so a fraction of a point compounds across locations. The goal is staffing matched to demand: lean enough to protect margin, deep enough to keep bays moving and tickets complete. myAnalyst shows labor hours and dollars next to sales in LiveStats, so you spot over- and under-staffing per store, not just chain-wide.

5. Revenue per hour

What it is

Net sales per labor hour (or operating hour): a productivity rate blending throughput and ticket size against the time it took to earn.

Why it matters

It is a fast read on how efficiently a store converts open hours into sales, exposing slow dayparts and overstaffed shifts that raw revenue hides. myAnalyst reports it in LiveStats alongside cars, labor, and avg ticket so the trade-offs sit side by side.

6. Repeat rate

What it is

The share of customers who come back: a direct measure of loyalty and the foundation of predictable, lower-cost revenue.

Why it matters

Winning a new customer costs far more than keeping one, and repeat customers carry higher lifetime value. myAnalyst's Retention module works at the VIN and household level, classifying customers as on-track, due-soon, overdue, or lapsed. So repeat rate becomes something you act on with due-for-service lists, not just measure after the fact.

7. Coupon ROI

What it is

The return on a promotion: incremental revenue it drove versus the discount and cost to run it.

Why it matters

Discounting is easy to start and hard to evaluate. Coupon ROI separates promotions that pull in new or lapsed customers from ones that just discount people who would have come anyway. myAnalyst tracks it in the Auto Care metric set so you compare campaigns across stores instead of guessing.

8. Bay productivity

What it is

Throughput and revenue per bay over a period.

Why it matters

Bays are fixed capacity. This tells you whether a store is using the plant it already has, or whether cars queue while bays sit idle. Read against car count and revenue per hour, it points to where staffing or process, not real estate, is the constraint. myAnalyst tracks it per location.

9. Technician efficiency

What it is

Work completed relative to hours worked.

Why it matters

It connects labor cost to output at the person level. You can see whether a strong store is strong because of staffing depth, training, or process, and which practices to spread to other locations. myAnalyst surfaces it alongside labor % and bay productivity so the picture is complete, not one metric in isolation.

10. Full-service mix

What it is

The share of visits with full-service work: inspections and services attached beyond the basic oil change.

Why it matters

It is the lever behind both ARO and average ticket. A store that consistently inspects and recommends the right services lifts revenue per car without more cars. Watching the mix per store shows where coaching on the inspect-and-recommend routine pays off. myAnalyst tracks full-service alongside net sales and cars in LiveStats.

11. Budget pace

What it is

Where a store stands against budget so far, projected to month-end: ahead of, on track with, or behind plan, not just the raw month-to-date total.

Why it matters

A month-to-date number with no shape tells you little; pace tells you whether today's trajectory lands above or below goal. myAnalyst's Pacing & Forecast uses linear and shape-adjusted projections with confidence bands, labels each store ahead / on-track / behind, and shows the dollars still needed. So you intervene mid-month instead of explaining a miss after it.

12. Store Health

What it is

A single 0 to 100 score and A to F grade per store that rolls the signals above into one number, built from five weighted pillars: Growth (25%), Goal (25%), Loyalty (20%), Standing (15%), and Operations (15%). Scores are calculated as chain-relative percentiles over a rolling 91-day window.

Why it matters

No operator can watch a dozen KPIs across dozens of stores daily. Store Health gives you one ranked view of where to look first, flags each location healthy, watch, or at-risk, tracks a 12-week trend, and surfaces the biggest movers. It is the fastest way to triage a portfolio. See exactly how the Store Health score works.

FAQ

Quick-lube KPI questions

What is a good average ticket for a quick lube?

There is no single right number. It depends on your service mix, pricing, market, and how much full-service work and upsell you do beyond the basic oil change. The useful comparison is your own trend and how each store ranks against the rest of your chain. myAnalyst tracks average ticket per store in real time with year-over-year deltas, so you see who is moving and why rather than chasing a rule of thumb.

What KPIs should a quick lube track?

The core set most multi-unit quick-lube operators run on: car count, average ticket, ARO (Average Repair Order), labor percentage, revenue per hour, repeat rate, coupon ROI, bay productivity, technician efficiency, full-service mix, budget pace, and an overall Store Health score that rolls these signals into a single grade. myAnalyst tracks each per store and updates them every five minutes from your POS.
