12 quick-lube KPIs
every operator should track.

A plain-English field guide to the oil-change and quick-lube metrics that actually move a multi-unit business, what each one means, why it matters, and how to keep it in front of you across every location.

Book a demo Auto-service analytics

Quick-lube is a fast, high-volume business: a car rolls in, a few decisions get made, and it rolls out, repeated hundreds of times a day across your shops. The operators who win across multiple locations are the ones who watch the same handful of numbers the same way at every store, every day. The twelve KPIs below are the ones multi-unit quick-lube and auto-service operators run on. For each one we cover what it means and why it matters, without inventing benchmark numbers, because the figure that matters is your own trend and how each store ranks against the rest of your chain.

myAnalyst reads these straight from your POS and refreshes them every five minutes, so you are comparing live reality store-to-store instead of rebuilding spreadsheets every morning. New to a term? The glossary defines the language used here.

1. Car count

What it is

The number of vehicles a store services in a given period. It is the most fundamental volume metric in quick-lube, everything else is a rate applied to this base.

Why it matters

Car count tells you whether a store's revenue swings come from traffic or from ticket size, and it is the first signal that something has changed in a market. In myAnalyst, car count appears live in LiveStats alongside net sales with year-over-year deltas, so a dip shows up the same day rather than at month-end.

2. Average ticket

What it is

Total net sales divided by car count, the average dollars per vehicle, across everything you sold that visit.

Why it matters

Average ticket is where service mix, pricing, and upsell discipline all land. Two stores can have identical car counts and very different revenue because of it. Track the trend per store and rank stores against each other rather than chasing an outside rule of thumb; myAnalyst surfaces average ticket per location with YoY change built in.

3. ARO (Average Repair Order)

What it is

The average value of a repair or service order. For quick-lube and auto service, ARO captures how much work, beyond the base oil change, a store is writing per vehicle.

Why it matters

ARO is the clearest measure of upsell and service-mix health. A rising ARO usually means technicians are inspecting, recommending, and closing the additional services a customer actually needs. It is one of the signature Auto Care metrics myAnalyst tracks, and you can read its definition in the glossary.

4. Labor %

What it is

Labor cost as a percentage of net sales, what you spend on people relative to what you bring in.

Why it matters

Labor is the largest controllable cost in most quick-lube shops, so even a fraction of a point compounds across many locations. The goal is staffing matched to demand: lean enough to protect margin, deep enough to keep bays moving and tickets complete. myAnalyst shows labor hours and labor dollars next to sales in LiveStats so you can see over- and under-staffing per store, not just chain-wide.

5. Revenue per hour

What it is

Net sales generated per labor hour (or per operating hour), a productivity rate that blends throughput and ticket size against the time it took to earn it.

Why it matters

Revenue per hour is a fast read on how efficiently a store converts open hours into sales. It exposes slow dayparts and overstaffed shifts that raw revenue hides. myAnalyst reports revenue per hour in LiveStats alongside cars, labor, and avg ticket so the trade-offs are visible side by side.

6. Repeat rate

What it is

The share of customers who come back for another visit, a direct measure of loyalty and the foundation of predictable, lower-cost revenue.

Why it matters

It costs far more to win a new customer than to keep an existing one, and repeat customers tend to carry higher lifetime value. myAnalyst's Retention module works at the VIN and household level, classifying customers as on-track, due-soon, overdue, or lapsed, so repeat rate is something you can act on with due-for-service lists, not just measure after the fact.

7. Coupon ROI

What it is

The return on a coupon or promotion: the incremental revenue it drove measured against the discount and cost to run it.

Why it matters

Discounting is easy to start and hard to evaluate. Coupon ROI separates promotions that pull in genuinely new or lapsed customers from ones that simply hand a discount to people who would have come anyway. myAnalyst tracks coupon ROI as part of the Auto Care metric set so you can compare campaigns across stores rather than guessing.

8. Bay productivity

What it is

How much work each service bay produces, throughput and revenue per bay over a period.

Why it matters

Bays are your fixed capacity. Bay productivity tells you whether a store is using the physical plant it already has, or whether cars are queuing while bays sit idle. Read against car count and revenue per hour, it points to where staffing or process, not real estate, is the constraint. It is part of the auto-service metric set myAnalyst tracks per location.

9. Technician efficiency

What it is

How productive technicians are, the work completed relative to the hours worked.

Why it matters

Technician efficiency connects labor cost to output at the person level. It helps you see whether a strong store is strong because of staffing depth, training, or process, and which practices are worth spreading to other locations. myAnalyst surfaces it alongside labor % and bay productivity so the picture is complete rather than one metric in isolation.

10. Full-service mix

What it is

The share of visits that include full-service work, the additional inspections and services attached to a visit beyond the basic oil change.

Why it matters

Full-service mix is the lever behind both ARO and average ticket. A store that consistently inspects and recommends the right services lifts revenue per car without needing more cars. Watching the mix per store shows you where coaching on the inspection-and-recommend routine will pay off; myAnalyst tracks full-service alongside net sales and cars in LiveStats.

11. Budget pace

What it is

Where a store stands against its budget so far this period, projected forward to month-end, not just the raw month-to-date total, but whether the store is ahead of, on track with, or behind plan.

Why it matters

A month-to-date number with no shape to it tells you little; pace tells you whether today's trajectory lands the store above or below goal. myAnalyst's Pacing & Forecast uses linear and shape-adjusted projections with confidence bands, labels each store ahead / on-track / behind, and shows the dollars still needed to hit the goal, so you can intervene mid-month instead of explaining a miss after it.

12. Store Health

What it is

A single 0 to 100 score and A to F grade per store that rolls the signals above into one number, built from five weighted pillars: Growth (25%), Goal (25%), Loyalty (20%), Standing (15%), and Operations (15%). Scores are calculated as chain-relative percentiles over a rolling 91-day window.

Why it matters

No operator can watch a dozen KPIs across dozens of stores every day. Store Health gives you one ranked view of where to look first, flags each location as healthy, watch, or at-risk, tracks a 12-week trend, and automatically surfaces the biggest movers. It is the fastest way to triage a portfolio, see exactly how the Store Health score works.

FAQ

Quick-lube KPI questions

What is a good average ticket for a quick lube?

There is no single right number, a healthy average ticket depends on your service mix, pricing, market, and how much full-service work and upsell you do beyond the basic oil change. The useful comparison is your own trend over time and how each store ranks against the rest of your chain. myAnalyst tracks average ticket per store in real time with year-over-year deltas, so you can see who is moving and why rather than chasing an industry rule of thumb.

What KPIs should a quick lube track?

The core set most multi-unit quick-lube operators run on includes car count, average ticket, ARO (Average Repair Order), labor percentage, revenue per hour, repeat rate, coupon ROI, bay productivity, technician efficiency, full-service mix, budget pace, and an overall Store Health score that rolls these signals into a single grade. myAnalyst defines and tracks each of these per store and updates them every five minutes from your POS.
